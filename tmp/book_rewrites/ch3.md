The storm broke properly as they left the community center.

No one walked together.

That had been Jonathan's rule, and no one argued: separate routes, separate transport, phones off unless using a fresh encrypted channel Emma created from a burner handset in front of them. They would reconvene in two hours at Diana's flat in Bloomsbury, a location Diana insisted she swept weekly for surveillance hardware.

At 20:14, all five arrived.

Diana's flat was narrow, book-lined, and clinically neat. Three white-noise machines sat humming at different frequencies. Her coffee table had been replaced with an improvised operations board: legal pads, index cards, marker pens, two laptops, and Eleanor Marsh's notebook under a desk lamp.

"Doors locked, phones in that metal box," Diana said. "If anyone objects, leave now."

No one objected.

Emma opened first.

"Verification results. Jonathan Blackwood was employed by Harrington Global until eighteen months ago. Peter Dalton did publish two financial investigations that were later discredited under unusual circumstances. Aisha Kazmi appears on current court filings in a confidential financial matter. Diana Mercer was sanctioned by UCL over ethics violations and has a documented brother, Richard Mercer, deceased in 2023."

She looked up.

"You all told the truth tonight. At least the parts we could verify quickly."

"Comforting," Peter said.

"Not enough," Jonathan answered.

He placed an encrypted drive on the table, the size of a thumb bone.

"This is the ledger archive. I didn't bring decryption keys."

Aisha folded her arms.

"Then we're still blind."

"Not entirely," Jonathan said. "I brought a print extract from memory. Five transaction lanes tagged MONARCH-1 through MONARCH-5. High-value transfers routed through diplomatic-protection structures."

Diana blinked.

"Diplomatic structures? Private finance doesn't get that without state capture."

"Exactly," Jonathan said.

Peter leaned over the page.

"What are they buying?"

"Not what," Emma said quietly. "Who."

She placed her mother's final letter beside the printout.

_They are protecting something called the Continuity Protocol._

The words sat on the table like an accusation.

Aisha read it again.

"Continuity could mean succession planning, data continuity, client retention architecture."

"Or continuity of identity," Diana said.

Everyone turned.

"Explain."

Diana tapped three times on Marsh's notebook where red lines connected dates, deaths, and name changes.

"In deception research, continuity operations preserve narrative selfhood across disruptions. New address, new employer, new history, same functional person. If weaponized at scale, you could erase legal identities and replace them with clean shells while preserving assets and influence."

Peter stared at her.

"You're saying they built industrial witness protection for the rich and criminal."

"I'm saying the pattern allows that interpretation," Diana said.

A hard knock hit the building's front door below.

Then another.

No one moved.

The intercom buzzed.

Diana didn't answer.

The buzzing stopped. Thirty seconds of silence followed. Then footsteps in the stairwell.

"How many flights up?" Jonathan whispered.

"Three," Diana said.

Emma killed the lamp. The room dropped into blue streetlight.

The footsteps paused outside the flat.

A key touched the lock.

Aisha swore softly.

"Back entrance?" Peter asked.

"Kitchen, fire stairs," Diana said.

The key turned once, failed, then tried again with pressure.

Jonathan grabbed the notebook and letter. Emma pocketed the drive. Peter took the burner phone and yanked the battery.

"Move," Aisha said.

They crossed the kitchen in darkness and spilled onto steel stairs slick with rain. Behind them came one heavy impact against Diana's front door, then a second that sounded like wood splitting.

They descended two flights before Diana stopped at a landing and looked up through the railwork.

"Two people," she whispered. "No uniforms. One is carrying a camera."

"They're documenting entry," Emma said. "Message operation."

At street level they split again by prearranged pairs: Jonathan with Aisha, Emma with Peter, Diana alone by choice.

Before they separated, Emma spoke once, voice almost lost in rain.

"No one goes home. No one uses their legal name. Meet point changes every hour until morning."

Peter met her eyes.

"We don't have a plan."

"We do now," Emma said. "Stay alive until sunrise."

Jonathan and Aisha turned east into traffic spray and red brake lights.

Emma and Peter cut west through an alley of shuttered cafes.

Diana stood for three seconds under a broken streetlamp, watching all four disappear.

Then she removed her SIM card with her thumbnail, snapped it in half, and walked north without looking back.

At 23:52, Emma sent one message to the burner group:

`They were in the flat before we finished first-pass verification.`

`This is not surveillance.`

`This is containment.`
