The offline machine belonged to Diana.

An old ThinkPad with its Wi-Fi card physically removed, Bluetooth module clipped, webcam taped, and ports epoxied except power and one USB input she'd installed herself. It booted from a write-protected system image she kept in a fireproof box under her sink.

At 05:02, in a borrowed basement room behind an all-night launderette, they broke the seal on the anti-static pouch.

"No one touches this except me," Diana said.

"Why you?" Peter asked.

"Because if this executes anything unexpected, I am currently the most replaceable." Her expression didn't change. "Also because I'm better at this than all of you."

No one argued.

She mounted the microSD through a hardware write blocker and ran a hash first.

"Static media," she said. "No obvious autorun artifacts."

Folders appeared.

`M1` `M2` `M3` `M4` `M5`

Inside each: ledger fragments, routing memos, legal opinion drafts, and image scans of identity dossiers with photographs redacted but metadata intact.

Aisha read one memo and went pale.

"These are legal privilege wrappers drafted to obstruct cross-border seizure. Whoever wrote this knew exactly how to survive regulator cooperation requests."

Jonathan pulled up a transaction CSV.

"These account signatures match the format I extracted before I ran. The ledger is real."

Peter enlarged one scanned cover sheet.

"Client Continuity Package: Tier Sovereign." He looked up. "This is a service catalog."

Emma's stomach turned cold.

"A catalog for replacing identities."

Diana opened another file.

`Session attendance matrix`

Five known names.

And one line marked only:

`Observer / Rotational Presence / Active`

No name. No photo. A schedule column instead, with timestamps overlapping their therapy sessions.

Peter stepped closer.

"That's our sixth member."

Aisha pointed to a note in the margin:

`Escalation condition: if Group M reaches evidence coherence threshold, trigger containment protocol.`

The basement room seemed to shrink.

"Evidence coherence threshold," Emma repeated. "That's what happened last night."

Jonathan sat back hard.

"They expected us to connect. Then they planned for it."

"Planned for this room too, maybe," Peter said.

He moved to the small basement window at street level and looked out through condensation.

"Blue van parked outside. Engine running. No logo."

Aisha crossed to him.

"Could be delivery."

"Could be us," Peter said.

Diana checked timestamps on the microSD files.

"Latest modification was four hours ago."

Everyone went still.

"Four hours?" Emma said.

"Yes. Around the time we were in Croydon."

Jonathan stared at the machine.

"Then the card was prepared for us."

Emma made the decision before the fear could settle.

"We still use it. Not as truth, as lead set. We verify every object against independent sources."

A sharp banging came from upstairs, metal on metal, like someone dropping a trolley.

Then voices.

Not close enough to parse.

Peter already had his bag on.

"We should move."

Diana copied the card to an encrypted volume and generated checksums.

"Done."

Aisha split the printouts: one set for legal mapping, one for publication prep, one for emergency dead-drop.

Emma burned a fresh burner and dictated the new rule set while Peter typed:

1. No fixed meeting location twice.
2. No digital transit without dual encryption.
3. No individual movement without shadow cover.
4. No claim accepted without two-source verification.
5. If contact is compromised, release packet alpha.

Jonathan looked at her.

"You sound like your mother."

"My mother thought therapy could outpace infrastructure," Emma said. "I'm not making that mistake."

They moved out through the launderette in staggered pairs.

Rain had stopped. Dawn bled into the sky above brick terraces and wet pavements.

For the first time in twelve hours, London looked ordinary.

That lasted until Emma reached the corner and found a folded note tucked under her wiper on the borrowed car.

No envelope. No fingerprints she could see.

One sentence in block capitals:

`SESSION 48 MOVED TO BLACKTHORNE HOUSE.`

Below it, a time.

`19:00`

And beneath that, five names, neatly printed.

All of theirs.

Peter read over her shoulder.

"That's not a threat," he said.

"No," Emma replied. "It's an invitation."

Aisha's voice was flat.

"To what?"

Emma folded the paper once.

"To prove we understood the rules."
