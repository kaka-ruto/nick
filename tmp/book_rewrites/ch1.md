Thomas arrived seven minutes early, as always.

The community center smelled of old carpet, instant coffee, and radiator heat turned a little too high. Fluorescent tubes buzzed overhead like trapped insects. He took his usual chair, third from the left, the one with the slight wobble, and aligned his notebook to the edges of his knees.

Diana was already there, legs crossed, coat folded with museum-level precision on the chair beside her.

"You're looking well," she said.

"No, I'm not," Thomas replied.

Their ritual. One lie. One correction. A handshake for people who trusted nobody.

Michael arrived next in a NASA flight jacket despite unseasonable warmth, carrying himself like a man entering a press conference. Last month he'd been ex-MI6. Before that, an orthopedic surgeon ruined by envy and politics.

"Mission control sends apologies for orbital delays," he announced to the empty room.

Aisha entered exactly on time, charcoal suit, perfect posture, unreadable expression. She placed a slim leather portfolio on her lap and scanned the circle once.

"Where's Dr. Marsh?"

"She called," Diana said. "Car trouble. Fifteen minutes."

Thomas checked his watch, then looked at Diana.

"Did she call?"

Diana gave him that half-smile that could mean amusement, contempt, or both.

"What do you think?"

Rebecca came in five minutes after start time with platinum hair that had been auburn last week and a paper plate covered in cling film.

"Lemon bars," she said. "My grandmother's recipe. Kentucky State Fair winner."

"Your grandmother died when you were an infant," Michael said, already taking one.

"Different grandmother," Rebecca said. "Also they're from Tesco." She paused. "But they are very good."

They were.

No one said that aloud.

They ate in a loose circle around Dr. Marsh's empty chair and kept pretending lateness was ordinary. Ten minutes became twenty. The buzz of lights felt louder.

"This is unlike her," Aisha said quietly.

"Maybe she graduated us," Michael said. "Sent us into the world with our improved pathology."

"I've been fully honest for forty-two minutes," Diana said.

"Session hasn't started," Thomas said.

"I included internal monologue," she replied.

Rebecca wasn't performing. Thomas noticed because she always was. But now she stared at the empty chair without blinking, one thumb worrying the edge of the cling film.

"Something's wrong," she said.

"You don't get to call normal," Aisha said.

Rebecca ignored her and looked directly at Thomas.

"I saw Marsh yesterday at Caffè Nero on High Street. She was meeting someone. They were arguing."

"Convenient," Michael said.

"I follow her sometimes," Rebecca said.

Aisha turned. "You _what_?"

"I follow everyone sometimes. Occupational hazard. She knows things about us we never said in here. I wanted to know where those things went."

The room went still.

Thomas felt a cold stitch under his ribs.

"Who was she meeting?" he asked.

"Man. Mid-fifties. Expensive suit. The sort of face that has private entrances." Rebecca reached into her bag. "She left this."

She placed a small leather notebook in her lap and opened it carefully.

Dr. Marsh's notebook.

Not session notes. Not feelings charts. Not treatment plans.

Names. Their names. Circles. Arrows. Dates. Addresses. Red marks beside some entries.

"That's not therapy," Aisha said.

"No," Rebecca said. "It looks like target mapping."

She turned to the last page.

A single line, written in Marsh's careful hand:

_One of them is telling the truth, and it will get me killed._

Rain began against the windows. Soft at first, then steady.

No one reached for another lemon bar.

"Could be role-play," Michael said, but he didn't sound convinced.

"Could be," Diana said. "Could also be evidence."

Thomas took the notebook, read the line again, and felt the room tilt half a degree.

"If this is real," he said, "then either Marsh found something and panicked, or someone wanted us to think she did."

"Either way she's missing," Rebecca said.

"Or she's avoiding us," Aisha said.

"She's never missed without notice," Rebecca said. "Not once."

"You track attendance now?" Diana asked.

"I track patterns."

Thomas looked at each of them in turn. Faces he'd watched lie with elegance, clumsiness, boredom, genius. Faces he'd trusted just enough to keep returning.

"Fine," he said. "Then we do the stupid obvious thing and tell the truth for once. Names. Work. Why we're actually here."

Diana leaned back.

"You first, Thomas. If that's your name."

Thomas held her eyes.

"It isn't," he said.

Lightning flashed behind the glass, briefly whitening every face in the room.

When darkness settled back in, no one looked the same.
