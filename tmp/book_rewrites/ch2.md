"My name is Jonathan Blackwood," Thomas said. "Not Thomas Elliot."

No one gasped. In a room built on invented biographies, names were the cheapest currency.

"I was an accountant at Harrington Global Investments," he continued. "Offshore structures, special-purpose vehicles, accounts that don't exist in the reports regulators see."

"Tax architecture," Aisha said.

"Sometimes. Mostly logistics for people who buy outcomes instead of services." Jonathan swallowed. "Money laundering, yes. Also deniable payments."

Michael leaned forward. "How close were you to the pipes?"

"Close enough to spot repetition where there shouldn't be any. Same intermediary entities, same legal wrappers, same midnight transfer windows. Then my colleague David asked a very specific question in the wrong meeting. Three days later he was gone."

"Gone as in resigned?" Diana asked.

"Gone as in presumed suicide. No body." Jonathan's hands trembled once, then stilled. "I started copying ledgers that week."

Rebecca tilted her head. "Blackmail or insurance?"

"Survival."

Rain drove harder against the glass.

"Why tell us now?" Aisha asked.

Jonathan pointed to Marsh's notebook.

"Because if she linked me to Harrington, she moved from therapist to liability."

Michael stood and paced two slow arcs.

"Or we are letting an elegant paranoid story become truth because it fits the mood."

"Then give us your truth," Rebecca said.

Michael stopped pacing.

"Peter Dalton. Investigative desk, The Guardian. Financial corruption and dark-money property flows." He looked at Jonathan. "I was building a piece on London shell ownership tied to Harrington facilitators. My editor killed it. My notes disappeared in a burglary that took nothing else. A week later I was fired for fabricating sources."

Diana exhaled through her nose.

"So you became visibly unreliable on purpose."

"Exactly. No one follows the clown into a spreadsheet."

Aisha looked at him for a long second.

"Or they do, if they are competent."

"Your turn," Jonathan said.

Aisha clasped her hands tighter.

"Aisha Kazmi. Barrister. Financial regulation and white-collar crime. I am working on a money-laundering prosecution strategy involving multiple institutions." She paused. "Harrington is central."

Peter gave a humorless laugh.

"There it is."

Diana crossed one leg over the other with deliberate calm.

"Diana Mercer. Former research psychologist, UCL. Deception science. I lost my post after an ethics investigation. I joined this group partly to continue observation and partly because I had a personal reason to stay close to this pattern of deaths."

"Personal reason?" Emma asked.

Diana's jaw tightened.

"My brother, Richard Mercer, died in what was ruled a suicide after raising concerns about banking compliance irregularities." She looked directly at Jonathan. "Harrington touched his case too."

No one spoke for several beats.

Jonathan broke first.

"That makes four direct links."

Emma nodded once.

"And my mother was mapping all four."

Peter looked at her. "You still haven't explained your role in this room."

Emma stared at the empty therapist chair.

"My legal name is Emma Marsh," she said. "Eleanor Marsh is my mother."

Michael cursed under his breath.

Aisha's voice went flat. "You embedded yourself as a patient."

"At her request. At first to observe you. Then because she thought someone here had the one factual thread that could pull the entire structure apart."

Jonathan leaned in.

"Is she missing?"

Emma held his gaze.

"No," she said. "She's dead."

The fluorescent lights hummed. A siren passed somewhere outside and faded.

"Police confirmed?" Aisha asked.

"Not publicly. They're holding at missing-person language while they process the scene. There was blood in her study. Signs of a struggle. Her laptop is gone."

Peter sat down slowly, as if his knees had changed their mind about supporting him.

"And you think one of us did it."

Emma unfolded a single sheet from her bag.

"I think one of us has been feeding information to whoever wanted her silenced."

She placed the page on the floor at the center of the circle. Five names. Five notes. One line circled in red:

_One of them is finally telling the truth._

Diana stared at it.

"That isn't clinical handwriting," she said.

"No," Emma said. "It's defensive writing."

Jonathan looked from face to face.

"Then we stop improvising," he said. "No more confession theater. We verify every claim with evidence tonight."

Peter nodded first.

"Agreed."

Aisha hesitated, then nodded.

Diana gave a small, bleak smile.

"At last. A protocol."

Emma reached for her coat.

"Good," she said. "Because if my mother was right, we are already late."
