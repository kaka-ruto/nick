At 00:18, Emma chose a twenty-four-hour pharmacy near King's Cross because it had three exits, mirrored glass, and a ceiling camera she could see from every aisle.

Peter arrived first, wet to the knees and carrying a paper bag with two cheap umbrellas, bottled water, and a disposable power bank.

"You look like someone who's been professionally chased," he said.

"I look like someone who's still alive," Emma said.

Aisha and Jonathan came in six minutes later from opposite sides of the street. Diana sent her location only after they were physically inside and visible on camera.

No one sat.

Emma drew columns on the back of a receipt with a pharmacy pen:

`Claim`  `Evidence`  `Risk if False`

"Verification night," she said. "No speeches."

Jonathan went first.

"Storage unit in Croydon was hit tonight before I got there. Lock replaced. CCTV cable cut."

"Evidence?" Emma asked.

He held up a photo of the cut coax and new lock plate.

"Risk if false?"

"I manipulated the scene to steer you."

Emma wrote it down.

Peter followed.

"My former editor took a call from a private number the night he killed my Harrington story. Phone logs erased from server backups but not from his old notebook export. I got a screenshot from a source in IT."

He passed his phone. A one-minute incoming call. No caller ID. Timestamp aligned with editorial decision.

"Risk if false?" Emma asked.

"I forged metadata."

Aisha laid two printed pages on a shelf between painkillers and vitamins.

"Kazmi Holdings, my family's investment vehicle, appears in a chain that intersects a MONARCH transaction intermediary. I confirmed via Companies House filings and a beneficial-ownership leak dataset."

Jonathan stared at her.

"You didn't know?"

"I suspected. I did not know." Aisha's voice stayed measured, but a muscle jumped in her jaw. "Risk if false: I'm laundering my family's exposure narrative in real time."

Diana arrived without greeting, removed damp gloves, and placed a memory card on the counter.

"Door-cam from opposite my building. Two entrants, one lookout, one vehicle. Plate partially visible."

"You had a door-cam opposite your building?" Peter asked.

"I had three," Diana said. "I only told you about zero." 

Emma almost smiled. Almost.

"Risk if false?"

"Deepfake composite."

Emma added the final line.

Silence.

Then she wrote a new heading beneath the columns:

`Working conclusion: independent claims cohere under shared pressure event.`

"Meaning," Jonathan said, "someone acted because we started aligning truth."

"Or someone acted to make us think that," Peter countered.

"Both can be true," Diana said. "Pressure and misdirection are complementary."

Emma unfolded Eleanor's final note again and tapped one sentence with her nail.

_There is a sixth member of your group who has never attended a session._

"Next step is identifying who had access to session context without physical attendance," she said.

Aisha frowned.

"That list includes your mother."

"My mother is dead." Emma's tone hardened. "Everyone else is on the table."

Jonathan looked at each of them.

"We need chain-of-custody on everything from this point. Timestamped copies, hash signatures, dead-man release."

Peter nodded.

"I can set the media side. If we die, packet goes to five desks and two offshore mirrors."

"Do it," Emma said.

At 01:07, the pharmacy lights flickered once.

Everyone looked up.

A cashier rolled his eyes and said to no one in particular, "Happens when the fridge compressor kicks in."

No one relaxed.

Diana turned her laptop and froze the frame from her memory card.

The partial plate was enough for pattern matching. Peter ran it against archived paparazzi and event footage tied to Harrington board members.

He went still.

"I know this car," he said.

"From where?" Aisha asked.

"Not a car. A fleet assignment." Peter zoomed in. "This plate sequence appears on security vehicles contracted to Blackthorne Protective Logistics."

Jonathan's face drained.

"Blackthorne handled internal security at Harrington headquarters."

"And external operations?" Emma asked.

"Unofficially, yes."

Diana looked back to Eleanor's note.

"Continuity Protocol plus private logistics plus diplomatic routing. This is not normal corporate crime." She met Emma's eyes. "This is infrastructure."

Aisha shut her folder.

"Then we stop behaving like witnesses and start behaving like targets."

Emma tore the receipt in half and distributed tasks.

"Jonathan, recover decryption keys. Not the drive, the keys."

"Peter, build release architecture and hostile-publication package skeleton."

"Aisha, map legal choke points and emergency injunction risks in UK and EU."

"Diana, profile likely controller behavior once they realize we changed operating pattern."

"I will pull my mother's offline archive and identify every reference to a sixth member."

Peter looked around the aisle, fluorescent white and antiseptic, as if seeing the scene from outside himself.

"When do we reconvene?"

Emma checked the time.

"Two hours. Different borough."

She paused, then added:

"And from now on, if anyone says 'trust me,' assume they're lying unless they can prove otherwise."

Diana zipped her coat.

"In this group," she said, "that's the first useful rule we've ever had."

They left one by one into the wet London dark, each taking a different direction, each carrying a fragment of a case no single person could survive alone.

At 01:29, Emma's burner vibrated once.

Unknown sender.

One line.

`Session attendance update: sixth member present.`
